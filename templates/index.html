Shariah Financial Metrics Calculator

Market value is essential in understanding how investors perceive the company in real-time. For Shariah-compliant companies, it’s not just about the market value in general, but also about ensuring that the business activities align with Islamic principles. Shariah compliance boards like AAOIFI (Accounting and Auditing Organization for Islamic Financial Institutions) provide guidelines to ensure that companies only engage in permissible activities. This includes avoiding investments in companies with large amounts of non-compliant income (like from alcohol or gambling). Shariah-compliant companies must demonstrate their market value while adhering to these standards, fostering trust and long-term reliability.

 

 

Frequently Asked: 

 

 

What if the business is not publically traded or not recently valuated?

 

For privately held businesses or those without recent valuations, the market value is less straightforward. However, business owners can still estimate the value using methods like comparative market analysis (comparing to similar businesses in the same industry) or discounted cash flow (DCF) analysis or Total Halal Assets. While these may not be as precise as market-driven valuation, they still provide a reasonable estimate of a company’s worth. For Shariah compliance, the key is ensuring that the business activities are halal, as discussed with the AAOIFI or IFSB guidelines. If a business is private, Shariah boards would still focus on the compliance of revenue streams and operational practices, rather than market fluctuations.

Total revenue is the total amount of money a company generates from its business activities, including the sale of goods or services, before any expenses are deducted. This metric is fundamental for understanding a company's ability to generate income and sustain growth. It’s often a top-line figure, indicating a company’s size and market demand. By tracking revenue over time, investors and analysts can assess how well a company is performing in its core operations. In Shariah-compliant screening, total revenue is particularly important as it serves as the denominator in key financial ratio tests—such as the Haram Revenue Ratio—used by recognized Shariah standards bodies like AAOIFI (Accounting and Auditing Organization for Islamic Financial Institutions) and MSCI Islamic Indexes. These organizations use revenue figures to determine what percentage of a company's income is derived from impermissible activities, helping investors make informed and ethical investment choices.

 

 

Frequently Asked: 

 

 

Is total revenue the same as profit?

 

No, total revenue is not the same as profit. Total revenue is the total amount of money a company earns from selling its products or services. Profit, on the other hand, is what’s left after the company subtracts all its expenses (like rent, salaries, and materials) from that revenue. So, revenue shows how much a company brings in, while profit shows how much it keeps.

Accounts receivable represents the money that a company is owed by its customers for products or services delivered on credit. It’s important because it shows how much revenue a company expects to receive in the future. However, high accounts receivable can indicate that a company might be struggling to collect payments, which can affect its liquidity. This is especially relevant from a Shariah compliance perspective, as boards like the Accounting and Auditing Organization for Islamic Financial Institutions (AAOIFI) and the Shariah standards followed by the MSCI Islamic Index Series and Dow Jones Islamic Market Index emphasize strong liquidity and low credit risk in their screening processes. Analyzing this metric gives insight into a company’s cash flow health and its effectiveness in managing credit risk, both of which are essential for upholding the financial discipline and ethical risk management encouraged in Islamic finance.

 

 

Frequently Asked: 

 

 

If accounts receivable is money the company hasn't received yet, why is it considered part of the company's income or value?

 

Accounts receivable is included as part of a company’s value because it reflects revenue that has already been earned under accrual accounting rules. Even though the cash hasn’t been collected yet, the company has delivered the goods or services, so it's legally entitled to that money. It’s considered an asset on the balance sheet because it represents future cash inflow. This helps investors understand the company’s short-term financial health and how well it manages payments from customers.

Debt refers to the total amount of money a company owes to external creditors. This metric includes loans, bonds, or other financial obligations the company has. Understanding a company’s debt load is crucial for evaluating its financial stability and risk. High levels of debt can indicate financial strain, but debt can also be used strategically for growth if managed well. Investors use this metric to assess how risky an investment in the company might be. From a Shariah compliance perspective, monitoring debt is especially critical. According to standards set by prominent Shariah boards such as the Accounting and Auditing Organization for Islamic Financial Institutions (AAOIFI) and the Shariah Supervisory Board (SSB) of MSCI and Dow Jones Islamic Market Indices, companies must maintain debt levels below a specific threshold—typically no more than 33% of total assets or market capitalization. This limit ensures the business does not overly rely on interest-based borrowing, which is prohibited in Islamic finance. Therefore, investors seeking Shariah-compliant opportunities rely heavily on this debt screening to ensure the company adheres to Islamic ethical guidelines regarding financial responsibility and interest avoidance.

 

 

Frequently Asked: 

 

 

Is all debt considered non-compliant in Islamic finance?

 

Not all debt makes a company non-compliant, but interest-based debt (riba) is the primary concern in Islamic finance. Shariah-compliant screening standards—such as those established by AAOIFI, MSCI Islamic Index, and Dow Jones Islamic Market Index—allow companies to carry a limited amount of conventional debt, typically not exceeding 33% of their total assets or market value. This threshold is used to strike a balance, recognizing that in many industries, some degree of conventional financing is hard to avoid. However, the goal is to ensure the company is not excessively reliant on interest-based financing.

Cash represents the liquid assets that a company has readily available to pay its bills, reinvest in the business, or return to shareholders. It’s a key metric for assessing a company’s short-term financial health and ability to weather economic challenges. A company with high cash reserves has more flexibility, but excessive idle cash can also suggest inefficiency in using available capital for growth or expansion. From a Shariah compliance perspective, as outlined by leading standards like AAOIFI (Accounting and Auditing Organization for Islamic Financial Institutions) and indices such as the Dow Jones Islamic Market Index (DJIM) and MSCI Islamic Index, cash must be carefully evaluated. Specifically, these standards assess whether the cash is deposited in interest-bearing (riba-based) accounts. If it is, and if such cash exceeds a certain threshold—typically 33% of total assets—it may render the company non-compliant. Therefore, both the amount and the nature of the cash holdings matter when screening a company for Islamic investment.

 

 

Frequently Asked: 

 

 

Why does it matter how much cash a company has—shouldn’t more cash always be a good thing?

 

While having cash is generally good because it means the company can cover its expenses and handle emergencies, too much idle cash may signal inefficiency. It might mean the company isn’t investing in growth opportunities or returning value to shareholders. In Islamic finance, it’s also important to know how that cash is held. If it’s stored in interest-bearing accounts, it could breach Shariah compliance guidelines like those set by AAOIFI and DJIM, making the company unsuitable for halal investing. So, both the amount and the source of cash matter.

Interest-bearing investment capital refers to the amount of money that a company has invested in financial instruments that generate interest, such as bonds or certificates of deposit. This is distinct from cash because it represents funds actively placed in interest-bearing vehicles rather than liquid assets available for immediate use. In Shariah-compliant financial screening—such as the standards set by AAOIFI (Accounting and Auditing Organization for Islamic Financial Institutions) and followed by global Shariah indices like Dow Jones Islamic Market Index (DJIM)—this category is highly scrutinized. These standards typically require that the full value of such investments (not just the interest income) be accounted for when evaluating a company’s compliance. That’s because any involvement in interest (riba) contradicts Islamic ethical principles. Therefore, understanding this metric is essential for identifying companies that may not align with Islamic investment guidelines, even if their income statements appear compliant on the surface.

 

 

Frequently Asked: 

 

 

Why is interest-bearing investment capital treated separately from cash, and why do we include the full value of the investment instead of just the interest income?

 

The full value of the investment is included because it represents the principal amount of the investment, which is a source of potential returns for the company. It's important to note that long-term investments, such as bonds or certificates of deposit, which are interest-bearing, are typically excluded from the cash category, as they are not immediately available for use. For Shariah-compliant investments, interest-bearing investments (riba) are not permissible. This is why it's crucial to separately track interest-bearing investment capital to ensure that it aligns with both Shariah compliance and liquidity management, allowing you to avoid any non-compliant assets that are not easily liquidated.

Haram Revenue refers to any income generated by activities or products that are prohibited under Islamic law. This could include, but is not limited to, revenue from gambling, alcohol, or interest-based financing (riba). Shariah-compliant financial institutions, such as the Accounting and Auditing Organization for Islamic Financial Institutions (AAOIFI) and the Islamic Financial Services Board (IFSB), emphasize the importance of identifying and excluding these revenues to maintain compliance with Islamic principles. For instance, companies that aim to operate in accordance with Shariah law must avoid engaging in businesses that generate haram revenue, as these activities violate the ethical guidelines set out by Shariah boards. This is especially important for companies that serve Muslim communities or investors, ensuring they uphold the moral and religious obligations of their stakeholders. Tracking haram revenue allows companies to ensure that they meet Shariah guidelines and demonstrate their commitment to ethical and lawful business practices.

 

 

Frequently Asked: 

 

 

What is haram revenue, and why is it important for a company to avoid it?

 

Haram revenue refers to money a company earns from activities that are prohibited in Islam, such as gambling, selling alcohol, or earning interest from loans (riba). It's important for companies, especially those seeking to operate in accordance with Shariah law, to avoid earning haram revenue. This ensures that their business practices are ethical and aligned with Islamic principles. By identifying and excluding haram sources of income, companies can maintain trust with Muslim investors and customers and adhere to Shariah guidelines set by organizations like AAOIFI and IFSB.
